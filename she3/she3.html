  • Course description

    Natural conversation is replete with seemingly incomplete questions and statements, such as "who for?" and "I will", and with remarks that are tangential to the main message being conveyed, as such the phrase "my boss" in the sentence "John, my boss, is coming here later". The utterances above exemplify ellipsis and parenthesis, respectively. Because ellipsis and parenthesis are used frequently in conversation, knowing what forms of ellipsis and parenthesis are available in a particular language is extremely useful for learners of that language. This seminar introduces students to the patterns of ellipsis and parenthesis that are observed in different varieties of English and explores some of the basic linguistic analyses offered for why these particular patterns -- and not others -- are observed.

    During the course, students will practice critiquing this literature via peer-lead seminars and will eventually submit an essay on ellipsis and/or parenthesis in which an original research contribution is encouraged.


  • Goals

    • To gain a detailed knowledge of the main types of elliptical and parenthetical constructions observed in contemporary spoken English.
    • To learn how arguments are constructed and supported in successful generative theorising.
    • To develop skills in reading the contemporary syntactic literature.
    • To improve academic researching, presenting, and writing skills.

    Attendance and seminar contributions
    The small amount of time allotted to seminars on this course (1.5 hours per week) dictates that, after the crash-course in syntactic theory that will occupy our first two weeks, seminars will usually involve summarising/synthesising the assigned weekly reading. Consequently, seminars provide our only opportunity to discuss as a group aspects of the literature that students find confusing, unconvincing, etc. Please be prepared to ask questions and contribute to seminar discussions. To ensure that our seminar time is productively spent, I urge students to email me one or two questions each about the reading assigned for that week. If you do decide to email, please send your email to me before 17:00 on Sunday, so that I have time to incorporate your questions into the upcoming Tuesday seminar. Please note that emailing questions is not obligatory, only very useful (to help me quantify students' level of engagement with the literature, to streamline seminar discussions, etc.).

    Presentation of reading
    Students will present a summary of an assigned reading. Students should extract and replicate in a short, bullet-pointed manner the contents of the paper. Running commentary and critique of the paper that is being summarised is encouraged. Presentations should be ~30 minutes long (minimum 25 minutes; maximum 40 minutes) and are informal in register. Handouts must be provided. Presentations will either occur in groups or individually, depending upon the class-size. Once finalised, the schedule for presentations will be available [here]. If group presentations are assigned, each member of the group will be given an individual grade. Each member of the group must speak. These presentations begin in week 7 and run until approximately week 11.

    Presentation of final essay
    In the final seminar (and maybe the penultimate seminar too), students will present a summary of their final essay. This assessment aims to replicate the environment of an academic conference. Students will prepare and present a maximally 15-minute-long slideshow of their final paper in a formal manner (using MS PowerPoint, Apple Keynote, LaTeX Beamer, Prezi, or any other presentation software) and will spend 5 minutes after their slideshow fielding questions from the audience. Once finalised, the schedule for presentations is available [here].

    Final essay [written in English only]
    Students will write an essay on a topic related to ellipsis and/or parenthesis. As a guideline, the essay should be no fewer than 15 pages (single-spaced A4, 2.5cm margins, size 12 font) and no longer than 25 pages (approximately 7000-8000 words). Students are free to research any topic related to ellipsis or parenthesis they wish. Students are not limited to discussing English, either. Some recommendations are provided in the bibliography section [here].

    Students must submit an outline of their final essay on 29.06.18, before 17:00, by email in either docx or pdf form. This outline must include all (sub)section headings (e.g. §1. Introduction, §2. Previous studies, etc.) and a brief summary of the intended contents of each (sub)section. Although it will not be graded, students are still obligated to submit an essay outline.

    Student must submit the final version of their essay on 17.08.18, before 17:00, by email in either docx or pdf form. Percentage points will be deducted for lateness (the later the essay is submitted, the more points are deducted).

    While student cooperation is otherwise encouraged, final essays must be undertaken individually. Students will be discouraged from working on very similar topics, and all essays will be subject to standard tests for plagiarism. Plagiarism, if discovered, will be dealt with in accordance with University of Konstanz's outstanding policies.

    Advice on how to write the final essay, plus the grading criteria, can be found [here].

  • Policies

    This class is meant to be stimulating, not overwhelming. So if you need help, please ask for it! Office-hours appointments can be made for any reason: to discuss material or requirements that are unclear, to discuss aspects of the literature that you find confusing, to discuss the progress (or lack of it) of your final paper. Although my official office hours are on Wednesday afternoon, I am open to making appointments for other times during the week. You can email me to arrange an appointment, or ask me in person straight after the seminar on Tuesday.

    I will endeavour to respond to your email within two working days, and to inform you of grades within two weeks of submission. Please be patient!

    If you wish to bring your laptop to class, please don't use it for making notes. Aside from competing with me for your attention, I find the sound of students typing rather distracting.
